# Write-up: OECD Greenhouse Gas Emissions Visualizations

## Paragraph 1 — Earnest Visualization

The first visualization addresses the question: *How have greenhouse gas emissions changed in major OECD countries since 1990?* The chart plots total greenhouse gas emissions (CO₂ equivalent), all sectors, for the United States, United Kingdom, Germany, Japan, and Canada over the full period 1990–2019. To support a proportional comparison across countries that differ greatly in absolute emissions, the data are transformed into an index with 1990 as the base year (1990 = 100). This normalization makes it possible to compare *rates of change* rather than raw levels, so that the progress of smaller emitters (e.g. the United Kingdom) can be read alongside that of larger ones (e.g. the United States) without the latter dominating the scale. The time horizon is left complete (1990–2019), and the y-axis is not truncated: it starts at zero and extends to the full range of the indexed values, avoiding any visual distortion of the magnitude of change. The data source is the OECD Environment Statistics (greenhouse gas emissions inventories).

## Paragraph 2 — Deceptive Visualization

The second visualization poses a different question: *Have U.S. emissions stabilized in the past decade?* The design emphasizes recent stability by focusing on the 2010–2019 period only, so that the longer-term rise and peak of U.S. emissions (e.g. in the mid-2000s) and the 1990 baseline are not visible. Raw totals in million tonnes are shown rather than an indexed series, so that year-to-year changes appear small relative to the overall scale. The y-axis is restricted to a narrow band (e.g. roughly 4,500–6,000 million tonnes), which compresses the vertical variation and makes the trend look flatter than it would on a full scale. A 3-year moving average is overlaid on the annual series, which smooths short-term volatility and further reinforces an impression of stability. The subtitle does not mention the exclusion of earlier decades or the choice of scaling.

## Paragraph 3 — Design Contrast

The two charts illustrate how design choices alter the story told by the same underlying dataset. In the earnest chart, indexing to 1990 = 100 makes proportional changes comparable across countries and makes large percentage reductions (e.g. in the United Kingdom and Germany) visually salient; in the deceptive chart, using absolute values on a truncated scale makes similar percentage changes look small. The long time horizon (1990–2019) in the first chart supports a narrative of long-term trends and divergence across countries; the short window (2010–2019) in the second supports a narrative of recent stability. Finally, the multi-country framing of the earnest chart invites comparison and context, while the single-country framing of the deceptive chart omits that context and focuses attention on a narrow slice of the data. In each case, the encoding and framing are technically defensible but lead to different interpretations.

## Paragraph 4 — Reflection

Although both visualizations rely on the same OECD dataset, differences in temporal scope, scaling, and framing substantially influence viewer interpretation. The earnest chart highlights proportional progress and long-run trends; the deceptive chart emphasizes a stable-looking recent decade. This exercise demonstrates how visualization choices—including the choice of transformation (index vs. raw totals), the choice of time window, and the choice of comparative context—can meaningfully shape public understanding of climate data even when the underlying numbers are consistent and correctly cited.
